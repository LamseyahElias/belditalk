# Module 7: Family & People — Talking About the People in Your Life

## Overview
Family is the center of Moroccan life. Within minutes of meeting someone, you'll be asked about your parents, siblings, and whether you're married. This module teaches you the vocabulary to talk about family, describe people, and navigate the deeply social world of Moroccan relationships.

## Dialogue 1: Getting to Know Someone

> **Moroccan:** Wash nta mzwej?
> **You:** La, ana 3azb. W nta?
> **Moroccan:** Ana mzwej, hamdullah. 3ndi jouj dyal l-wlad.
> **You:** Tbark Allah! Wlad wla bnat?
> **Moroccan:** Wahed weld w wehda bent. W nta, 3ndek khouya wla khtek?
> **You:** 3ndi jouj dyal l-khout — wahed khouya w wehda khti.
> **Moroccan:** 3a2ila mezyana!

**Translation:**
> **Moroccan:** Are you married?
> **You:** No, I'm single. And you?
> **Moroccan:** I'm married, thanks to God. I have two kids.
> **You:** God bless! Boys or girls?
> **Moroccan:** One boy and one girl. And you, do you have a brother or sister?
> **You:** I have two siblings — one brother and one sister.
> **Moroccan:** Nice family!

## Dialogue 2: Talking About Parents

> **You:** Fin sakna 3a2iltek?
> **Moroccan:** Mama w baba f Fes. Jddi w jdda f l-blad, f wahed douar sghir.
> **You:** W nta, fin sakin?
> **Moroccan:** Ana f Casa, m3a mrati w l-wlad.
> **You:** Wash katmshi l-Fes bezzaf?
> **Moroccan:** Iyeh, kol jom3a n7awel nmshi. Mama katraq li l-makla, w l-wlad kayb3o jddhom.
> **You:** Hadi haja zwina bezzaf.

**Translation:**
> **You:** Where does your family live?
> **Moroccan:** Mom and dad in Fez. My grandparents in the countryside, in a small village.
> **You:** And you, where do you live?
> **Moroccan:** I'm in Casa, with my wife and kids.
> **You:** Do you go to Fez often?
> **Moroccan:** Yes, every week I try to go. Mom cooks for me, and the kids love their grandpa.
> **You:** That's really beautiful.

## Dialogue 3: Meeting the Family

> **Host:** Aji, dkhol! Hadi mama, w hada baba.
> **You:** Salam 3alaykom! Mrhba, labas 3likom?
> **Mother:** Wa 3alaykom salam! Mrhba bik, a wldi. Labas, hamdullah. Tfddal, gles!
> **You:** Shukran bezzaf, a khalti.
> **Father:** Mrhba, mrhba. Nta sahb wldi?
> **You:** Iyeh, sahbo men l-khdma.
> **Mother:** Tfddal atay! Kol, kol, ma tstenash!

**Translation:**
> **Host:** Come, enter! This is my mom, and this is my dad.
> **You:** Peace be upon you! Welcome, how are you?
> **Mother:** And upon you peace! Welcome, my child. Fine, thanks to God. Please, sit!
> **You:** Thank you so much, auntie.
> **Father:** Welcome, welcome. You're my son's friend?
> **You:** Yes, his friend from work.
> **Mother:** Have some tea! Eat, eat, don't be shy!

## Dialogue 4: Describing People

> **You:** Shkoun hadak?
> **Friend:** Hadak? Hada khouya l-kbir, Youssef. Howa mzwej, 3ndo tlata dyal l-wlad.
> **You:** W hadik li m3ah?
> **Friend:** Hadik mratu, Fatima. Hiya sahbat khti.
> **You:** W dak rajl l-kbir?
> **Friend:** Hada jddi. 3ndo 80 3am, w mazal b7alo!
> **You:** Tbark Allah 3lih!

**Translation:**
> **You:** Who is that?
> **Friend:** That one? That's my older brother, Youssef. He's married, has three kids.
> **You:** And that woman with him?
> **Friend:** That's his wife, Fatima. She's my sister's friend.
> **You:** And that older man?
> **Friend:** That's my grandfather. He's 80 years old, and still going strong!
> **You:** God bless him!

## Cultural Notes

### 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family is Everything
In Morocco, family isn't just important — it's the foundation of social life. Many adults live with their parents until marriage (and sometimes after). Grandparents are revered. Asking about someone's family shows genuine interest and builds trust.

### 🗣️ "Khouya" and "Khti" as Social Lubricant
Moroccans call strangers "khouya" (brother) and "khti" (sister) constantly. A taxi driver, shopkeeper, or random person on the street — everyone is "khouya." It creates instant warmth. Use it freely.

### 💍 The Marriage Question
"Wash mzwej?" (Are you married?) will come up within minutes of meeting someone. It's not intrusive — it's normal small talk. Don't be offended. If you're single, expect to hear "Inshallah qriban!" (God willing, soon!).

### 👵 Respect for Elders
Elders are treated with deep respect. You kiss their hand or forehead as a greeting. You don't argue with them publicly. When jddi speaks, everyone listens.

### 🏠 "Khalti" for Older Women
Even if she's not your aunt, any older woman can be called "khalti" (auntie). It's a sign of respect and affection. Similarly, "3mmi" for older men you're not related to.

## Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: Fill in the Blanks
1. _______ w _______ saknin f Rabat. (My mom and dad live in Rabat.)
2. 3ndi wahed _______ w jouj dyal _______. (I have one brother and two sisters.)
3. _______ mzwej w 3ndo tlata dyal l-_______. (My uncle is married and has three kids.)
4. Wash nta _______? — La, ana _______. (Are you married? — No, I'm single.)
5. _______ 3ndha 90 3am, tbark Allah. (My grandmother is 90 years old, bless her.)

**Answers:** 1. Mama, baba  2. khouya, l-khwatate  3. 3mmi, wlad  4. mzwej, 3azb  5. Jdda

### Exercise 2: Translate to English
1. "3a2ilti kbira — 3ndi khams dyal l-khout."
2. "Mrati smitha Nadia, hiya men Meknes."
3. "Jddi w jdda saknin m3ana f dar."
4. "Benti sghira, 3ndha 3 snin."
5. "Sahbi hada, kaykhdm m3aya."

**Answers:**
1. My family is big — I have five siblings.
2. My wife's name is Nadia, she's from Meknes.
3. My grandfather and grandmother live with us at home.
4. My daughter is little, she's 3 years old.
5. This is my friend, he works with me.

### Exercise 3: Role-Play — Meeting Someone New
You're at a cafe and strike up a conversation. Practice:

2. Ask about their family (parents, siblings, kids)
3. Describe your own family
4. Use "khouya" or "khti" naturally
5. React to their answers with "Tbark Allah" or "Hamdullah"

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Using "Khouya" with Women
"Khouya" is for males. For females, use "khti." Mixing these up sounds very awkward.

### ❌ Forgetting Possessive Forms
"Khouya" already means "my brother" (khou + ya). Don't say "dyali khouya." The "ya/i" ending already indicates possession: khti (my sister), mrati (my wife), rajli (my husband).

### ❌ Asking Women If They're Married
While "Wash mzwej?" is common between men, asking a woman "Wash mzwja?" can be awkward if you've just met. Let it come up naturally, or ask through conversation context.

### ❌ Confusing "Wlad" and "Bent"
"Wlad" can mean both "children" (general) and "boys" specifically. Context matters. "Wladi" = my children. "L-wlad" = the boys/kids. "Bent" is always specifically a girl/daughter.

### ❌ Not Greeting Everyone
When you enter a room with family, greet EVERY person individually. Don't just wave at the group. Go around, shake hands (or cheek-kiss if appropriate), and say "salam" to each person. Skipping someone is rude.
