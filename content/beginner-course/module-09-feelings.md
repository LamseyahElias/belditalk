# Module 9: Feelings & Opinions — Express Yourself in Darija

## Overview
Moving beyond basic transactions, this module teaches you how to express what you feel, what you like, and what you think. Being able to say "I'm happy," "I'm tired," or "I love this!" transforms you from a tourist reading phrases to someone actually connecting with people. This is where real conversations begin.

## Dialogue 1: How Are You Really Feeling?

> **Friend:** Labas 3lik? Kol shi bikhir?
> **You:** Wallah, lyoum 3yyan shwiya. Ma neastsh mezyan lbareh.
> **Friend:** 3lash? Moshkil?
> **You:** La, ghir bard f l-lil w ma qdertsh nna3es.
> **Friend:** Mskeen! Aji, nshrbou atay w nrta7ou shwiya.
> **You:** Fikra mezyana. Shukran a sahbi.

**Translation:**
> **Friend:** Are you okay? Everything alright?
> **You:** Honestly, today I'm a bit tired. I didn't sleep well last night.
> **Friend:** Why? A problem?
> **You:** No, just cold at night and I couldn't sleep.
> **Friend:** Poor thing! Come, let's drink tea and relax a bit.
> **You:** Good idea. Thanks, my friend.

## Dialogue 2: Expressing Opinions

> **You:** 3jbni had l-blasa bezzaf! Zwina!
> **Friend:** Iyeh, Chefchaouen kol shi zwin fiha.
> **You:** L-makla hna mzyana bezzaf. W n-nas mzyinin.
> **Friend:** W l-wqt dyal lyoum — la mskhon la bard. Wahed nhar zwin.
> **You:** Bghit nb9a hna! Ma bghitsh nrj3 l-Casa.
> **Friend:** Hahaha, mrhba bik! Walakin l-khdma katstenna.
> **You:** Iyeh... m3ttel men l-khdma.

**Translation:**
> **You:** I love this place so much! Beautiful!
> **Friend:** Yes, everything in Chefchaouen is beautiful.
> **You:** The food here is really good. And the people are great.
> **Friend:** And today's weather — not hot, not cold. A beautiful day.
> **You:** I want to stay here! I don't want to go back to Casa.
> **Friend:** Haha, you're welcome! But work is waiting.
> **You:** Yeah... fed up with work.

## Dialogue 3: At a Restaurant

> **Waiter:** Kol shi bikhir? 3jbkom l-makla?
> **You:** Iyeh, l-tajine mzyan bezzaf! 3jbni.
> **Friend:** W l-couscous dyal had l-blasa — tbark Allah.
> **You:** Walakin l-harira barda shwiya.
> **Waiter:** Oh, smeh lik! Ghadi njib lik wehda skhona.
> **You:** La la, safi, shukran. Mashi moshkil kbir.
> **Friend:** Howa dima kayb9a farhan w mzyan m3a z-zboun. Blasa mezyana hadi.

**Translation:**
> **Waiter:** Everything alright? Did you like the food?
> **You:** Yes, the tajine is very good! I love it.
> **Friend:** And the couscous at this place — amazing.
> **You:** But the harira is a bit cold.
> **Waiter:** Oh, sorry! I'll bring you a hot one.
> **You:** No no, it's fine, thanks. Not a big problem.
> **Friend:** He's always happy and good with customers. This is a great place.

## Cultural Notes

### 😊 Moroccans Default to Positive
When asked "Labas?" most Moroccans will say "Labas, hamdullah" even if they're having a terrible day. Complaining publicly is uncommon. Close friends get the real answer, but strangers get the polite version.

### 💪 "3yyan" Is Both Tired AND Sick
Context matters a lot here. "Ana 3yyan" can mean "I'm tired" or "I'm sick." If someone says it, ask "Mrid wla ghir 3yyan?" (Sick or just tired?) to understand.

### ❤️ "Bghit" — Want AND Love
"Bghit" is one of the most versatile words in Darija:
- "Bghit atay" = I want tea
- "Kanbghik" = I love you (romantic)
- "Kanbghi l-Maghrib" = I love Morocco
The "kan-" prefix makes it present continuous, and "-k" makes it "you."

### 🎨 "Zwin/Zwina" Is the Ultimate Compliment
Calling something "zwin" or "zwina" is the go-to Moroccan compliment. Use it for people, places, food, weather, clothes — anything beautiful or pleasing.

### 😤 Expressing Frustration Politely
Moroccans might say "m3ttel" (annoyed) rather than using strong language. In public, keeping composure is valued. Sarcasm and humor are preferred over direct complaints.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Forgetting Gender Agreement
Adjectives change based on who you're describing:
- Male: farhan, 3yyan, hzin, j3an
- Female: farhana, 3yyana, hzina, j3ana
If you're a woman, use the feminine forms for yourself.

### ❌ Using "Bghit" When You Mean "3jbni"
"Bghit" = I want/love (active desire). "3jbni" = I like/am impressed by (reaction). Don't say "bghit had l-manzar" (I want this view) when you mean "3jbni had l-manzar" (I like this view).

### ❌ Overusing "Khayb"
"Khayb" (bad/ugly) is a strong word. For mild criticism, use "mashi mzyan bezzaf" (not very good) instead. Calling someone's cooking "khayb" would be genuinely offensive.

### ❌ Saying "Krhit" About People
"Krhit" (I hate) is very strong when directed at people. Use it for things (traffic, heat, crowds) but be careful with people. "Ma 3jbnish" (I don't like) is much softer.

### ❌ Not Adding "Hamdullah"
After saying you're doing well, always add "hamdullah" (thanks to God). Saying "Ana bikhir" without "hamdullah" sounds incomplete and potentially ungrateful to Moroccan ears.
