# Module 8: Time & Days — Navigating the Moroccan Schedule

## Overview
Time in Morocco has its own rhythm. Shops open late, dinner happens at 10pm, and "I'll be there in 5 minutes" might mean 30. This module teaches you how to talk about time, days of the week, and parts of the day — plus how Moroccan time culture actually works so you're never caught off guard.

## Dialogue 1: Making Plans

> **You:** Ash ghadi ndir ghdda?
> **Friend:** Ghdda? Nhar l-jm3a... nmshiw l-souk f s-sbah?
> **You:** F shhal?
> **Friend:** F t-ts3od dyal s-sbah, wakha?
> **You:** Ts3od bkri shwiya... 3shra?
> **Friend:** Wakha, 3shra mezyan. Ntsennawk f l-qhwa.
> **You:** Safi, nshofek ghdda f 3shra.

**Translation:**
> **You:** What are we doing tomorrow?
> **Friend:** Tomorrow? It's Friday... let's go to the souk in the morning?
> **You:** At what time?
> **Friend:** At 9 in the morning, okay?
> **You:** 9 is a bit early... 10?
> **Friend:** Okay, 10 is good. I'll wait for you at the cafe.
> **You:** Done, see you tomorrow at 10.

## Dialogue 2: Asking the Time

> **You:** Smeh li, shhal f s-sa3a?
> **Stranger:** S-sa3a? Tlata w nos.
> **You:** Shukran. W fin kayn shi pharmacie qriba?
> **Stranger:** Kayna wehda, walakin tsddat f tlata. Rj3i ghdda f s-sbah.
> **You:** F shhal kaytefteh?
> **Stranger:** F t-tmnya dyal s-sbah.
> **You:** Wakha, shukran bezzaf.

**Translation:**
> **You:** Excuse me, what time is it?
> **Stranger:** The time? 3:30.
> **You:** Thanks. And is there a pharmacy nearby?
> **Stranger:** There's one, but it closed at 3. Come back tomorrow morning.
> **You:** What time does it open?
> **Stranger:** At 8 in the morning.
> **You:** Okay, thank you very much.

## Dialogue 3: Being Late

> **Friend:** Fin nta? S-sa3a rb3a w nos!
> **You:** Smeh li, ana m3ttel! Traffic bezzaf lyoum.
> **Friend:** Dima m3ttel, a sahbi!
> **You:** Ghadi nwsel daba daba, tsenna 3afak. 10 dqayq.
> **Friend:** Wakha, walakin 3awed tkoun f l-wqt, 3afak!
> **You:** Inshallah, lmerra jay!

**Translation:**
> **Friend:** Where are you? It's 4:30!
> **You:** Sorry, I'm late! Too much traffic today.
> **Friend:** Always late, my friend!
> **You:** I'll arrive right now, wait please. 10 minutes.
> **Friend:** Okay, but next time be on time, please!
> **You:** God willing, next time!

## Cultural Notes

### ⏰ Moroccan Time (It's Flexible)
In Morocco, time is more of a suggestion than a rule. If someone says "I'll be there at 3," expect them between 3:15 and 4:00. This isn't considered rude — it's just how social time works. Only formal events (flights, official appointments) run on strict schedules.

### 🕌 Friday is Special
Friday (Jm3a) is the Islamic holy day. Many men attend Friday prayer at the mosque around noon. Some shops close for a few hours in the early afternoon. It's not a full day off, but the rhythm of the day changes.

### 🌙 Late Nights Are Normal
Moroccans eat dinner late (9-11pm), especially in summer. Cafes are packed at midnight. Families walk together at 11pm. Don't be surprised when a shopkeeper is open at 10pm — that's normal.

### 🕐 Two Time Changes
Morocco follows GMT but switches to GMT+1 during most of the year, then goes back to GMT during Ramadan. This causes confusion even for locals. Always double-check time during Ramadan transitions.

### ☀️ Parts of the Day
- **S-sbah** (morning): roughly 6am-12pm
- **D-dhor** (midday): 12pm-3pm
- **3shiya** (afternoon/evening): 3pm-sunset
- **L-lil** (night): after sunset

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Confusing "Ghdda" and "Lbareh"
"Ghdda" = tomorrow, "lbareh" = yesterday. Mixing them up leads to total confusion. Memory trick: **G**hdda = **G**oing forward (tomorrow).

### ❌ Saying "Shhal sa3a?" Instead of "Shhal f s-sa3a?"
The correct way to ask time is "Shhal f s-sa3a?" (What is in the clock?). Dropping the "f" sounds unnatural.

### ❌ Taking "Daba" Too Literally
"Daba" means "now," but "daba nji" (I'll come now) often means "I'll come soon-ish." "Daba daba" (now now) is closer to actually meaning right now.

### ❌ Using French Days Instead of Darija
Many Moroccans mix French day names in casual speech ("lundi, mardi..."), but learning the Darija days shows effort and earns respect. Plus, older Moroccans and those outside cities prefer Darija.

### ❌ Expecting Punctuality
Don't get frustrated when Moroccans are "late." The cultural concept of time is different. Build in buffer time for social meetings. Business meetings tend to be more punctual.
