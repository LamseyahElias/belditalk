# Module 11: Culture & Customs — The Phrases That Connect You to Moroccan Life

## Overview
Language without culture is just sounds. This module teaches you the Islamic and Moroccan expressions woven into daily life — the phrases people say without thinking but that mean everything. Mastering these will make Moroccans look at you differently: not as a foreigner speaking Darija, but as someone who truly understands their world.

## Dialogue 1: Arriving at a Moroccan Home

> **Host:** Mrhba, mrhba! Ahlan bik! Tfddal, dkhol!
> **You:** Shukran, Lah ybarek fik.
> **Host:** Bismillah, gles. Rani ghadi njib lik atay.
> **You:** Lah y3tik s-s7a!
> **Host:** Allah yselmek. Hak, tfddal.
> **You:** Bismillah. *(sips tea)* Mmm, bnin bezzaf!
> **Host:** Bsaha!
> **You:** Allah y3tik s-s7a, had atay zwin bezzaf.
> **Host:** Bla jmil, a wldi. Hada darek.

**Translation:**
> **Host:** Welcome, welcome! Come in!
> **You:** Thank you, God bless you.
> **Host:** In God's name, sit. I'll bring you tea.
> **You:** God give you health! (thanks for the effort)
> **Host:** God keep you safe. Here, go ahead.
> **You:** In God's name. *(sips tea)* Mmm, very delicious!
> **Host:** To your health!
> **You:** God give you health, this tea is wonderful.
> **Host:** You're welcome, my child. This is your home.

## Dialogue 2: At a Celebration

> **You:** 3id Mubarak!
> **Neighbor:** 3id Mubarak! Mrhba bik! Aji, kol m3ana.
> **You:** Shukran bezzaf! Lah ybarek fik.
> **Neighbor:** Bismillah, kol! Had l-7lowat dyalna.
> **You:** *(eating)* Tbark Allah! Bnin bezzaf, shkoun li tyyebhom?
> **Neighbor:** Mama li tyyebhom. 3ndha 70 3am w mazal kat-tyyeb kol shi.
> **You:** Tbark Allah 3liha! Lah y7fdhha.
> **Neighbor:** Amin, Lah ybarek fik.

**Translation:**
> **You:** Happy Eid!
> **Neighbor:** Happy Eid! Welcome! Come, eat with us.
> **You:** Thank you so much! God bless you.
> **Neighbor:** In God's name, eat! These are our sweets.
> **You:** *(eating)* Wow! So delicious, who made them?
> **Neighbor:** My mom made them. She's 70 and still cooks everything.
> **You:** God bless her! May God preserve her.
> **Neighbor:** Amen, God bless you.

## Dialogue 3: During Ramadan

> **You:** Ramadan Mubarak, a sahbi!
> **Friend:** Ramadan Mubarak! Wash kat-soum?
> **You:** Iyeh, hamdullah, hada awal Ramadan li kn-soum.
> **Friend:** Tbark Allah 3lik! Ma sha Allah!
> **You:** Walakin s3ib shwiya... j3an w 3tshan.
> **Friend:** Normal f l-awal. Men b3d kat-twellef. Aji, ftour 3ndna lyoum.
> **You:** Bssah? Shukran bezzaf!
> **Friend:** Bla jmil. Saha ftourek mqeddem!
> **You:** Lah ybarek fik.

**Translation:**
> **You:** Blessed Ramadan, my friend!
> **Friend:** Blessed Ramadan! Are you fasting?
> **You:** Yes, thanks to God, this is my first Ramadan fasting.
> **Friend:** God bless you! Amazing!
> **You:** But it's a bit hard... hungry and thirsty.
> **Friend:** Normal at first. After a while you get used to it. Come, break fast at our place tonight.
> **You:** Really? Thank you so much!
> **Friend:** You're welcome. Blessed iftar in advance!
> **You:** God bless you.

## Dialogue 4: The Tea Ceremony

> **Host:** Atay wla qhwa?
> **You:** Atay, 3afak.
> **Host:** *(preparing tea with ceremony — rinsing glasses, pouring high)*
> **You:** 3lash kat-sobb men l-fouq hakda?
> **Host:** Bach t-jib r-rghwa! Atay bla rghwa mashi atay.
> **You:** Ah, fhemt! W bshhal dyal s-sokkar?
> **Host:** Bezzaf! Hna f l-Maghrib, atay khasso ykoun 7lo.
> **You:** *(drinks)* Bismillah... Tbark Allah, bnin bezzaf!
> **Host:** Bsaha! Zid kas akhor?
> **You:** Iyeh, 3afak! Lah y3tik s-s7a.

**Translation:**
> **Host:** Tea or coffee?
> **You:** Tea, please.
> **Host:** *(preparing tea with ceremony — rinsing glasses, pouring high)*
> **You:** Why do you pour from up high like that?
> **Host:** To make the foam! Tea without foam isn't tea.
> **You:** Ah, I see! And how much sugar?
> **Host:** A lot! Here in Morocco, tea has to be sweet.
> **You:** *(drinks)* In God's name... Wow, so delicious!
> **Host:** To your health! Another glass?
> **You:** Yes, please! God give you health.

## Cultural Notes

### 🤲 These Phrases Aren't Just Religious — They're Cultural
Even non-practicing Moroccans use "Inshallah," "Bismillah," and "Hamdullah" constantly. They're cultural reflexes, not necessarily religious statements. Using them doesn't require any particular belief — it shows cultural awareness.

### 🍵 The Moroccan Tea Ceremony
Moroccan mint tea (atay b-na3na3) is poured from a height to create foam (rghwa), which is considered essential. Tea is always very sweet. Refusing tea is considered somewhat impolite — at least accept one glass. The host will always offer "Zid?" (More?). Three glasses is traditional.

### 👁️ The Evil Eye (L-3in)
"Tbark Allah" is said before complimenting someone to protect them from the evil eye (l-3in). If you admire a baby and forget to say "Tbark Allah," the mother might worry. Always pair compliments with this phrase.

### 🌙 Ramadan Etiquette
During Ramadan, most Moroccans fast from dawn to sunset. Even if you're not fasting, avoid eating, drinking, or smoking in public out of respect. Restaurants may be closed during the day. Iftar (breaking the fast) is usually harira soup, dates, milk, and chebakia sweets.

### 🏠 "Hada Darek" — This is Your Home
When visiting a Moroccan home, the host will say "Hada darek" (This is your home). It means: feel comfortable, you belong here. The correct response is "Lah ybarek fik" (God bless you).

### 🤧 Sneezing Protocol
When someone sneezes: you say **"Lah yrhmek"** (God have mercy on you). They respond **"Lah yghfer lik"** (God forgive you). It's automatic — forgetting is noticed.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Saying "Inshallah" About the Past
"Inshallah" is ONLY for the future. Don't say "Lbareh mshit l-souk, inshallah" — you already went! It's used for things that haven't happened yet.

### ❌ Forgetting "Tbark Allah" Before Compliments
Saying "Your baby is so beautiful!" without "Tbark Allah" first can make parents uneasy. Always lead with it: "Tbark Allah, benti zwina bezzaf!"

### ❌ Refusing Tea
In most contexts, refusing tea is seen as declining hospitality. Even if you don't want a full glass, accept it, sip a little, and be gracious. You can say "Ghir shwiya" (just a little).

### ❌ Eating Before "Bismillah"
In any group meal, wait for the host or eldest person. The meal begins when someone says "Bismillah." Eating before this is like starting before grace at a formal Western dinner.

### ❌ Using "Inshallah" as "No"
While some people joke that "Inshallah" means "it's not going to happen," using it sarcastically is disrespectful. Mean it when you say it, or use a direct "Waqila" (maybe) or "Nshuf" (I'll see) if you're unsure.
