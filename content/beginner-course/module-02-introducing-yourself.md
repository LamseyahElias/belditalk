# Module 2: Introducing Yourself

## Overview
Now that you can greet people, it's time to tell them who you are! In this module, you'll learn how to share your name, where you're from, your age, and what you do. These are the building blocks of every first conversation in Morocco.

### Key Grammar: How Darija Sentences Work
Darija is simpler than standard Arabic. There's no "am/is/are" — you just drop it:
- English: "I **am** from America" → Darija: "Ana mn Amrika" (literally: "I from America")
- English: "My name **is** Sarah" → Darija: "Smiti Sarah" (literally: "My name Sarah")

## Dialogue 1: Meeting at a Café

> **Moroccan:** Salam! Labas? Shkoon nta? (Who are you?)
> **You:** Salam! Ana James. Smiti James.
> **Moroccan:** Mrhba, James! Mnin nta?
> **You:** Ana mn Amrika. W nta?
> **Moroccan:** Ana mghribi, mn Casablanca.

**Translation:**
> **Moroccan:** Hello! How are you? Who are you?
> **You:** Hello! I'm James. My name is James.
> **Moroccan:** Welcome, James! Where are you from?
> **You:** I'm from America. And you?
> **Moroccan:** I'm Moroccan, from Casablanca.

---

## Dialogue 2: Full Introduction

> **You:** Salam 3alaykom! Smiti Sarah. Ana mn London.
> **Moroccan:** Wa 3alaykom salam! Mrhba! Ash katdir f London?
> **You:** Khddama f wahed l'hospital. Ana doctora.
> **Moroccan:** Tbark Allah! W katkallmi l3arbiya?
> **You:** Shwiya d darija. Mazal kantel3em. (I'm still learning)

**Translation:**
> **You:** Peace be upon you! My name is Sarah. I'm from London.
> **Moroccan:** And upon you peace! Welcome! What do you do in London?
> **You:** I work in a hospital. I'm a doctor.
> **Moroccan:** God bless! And you speak Arabic?
> **You:** A little Darija. I'm still learning.

---

## Dialogue 3: Age and Family

> **Moroccan:** Shhal f 3mrek? (How old are you?)
> **You:** 3ndi tlatin 3am. (I'm 30 years old)
> **Moroccan:** W mzuwwj? (And married?)
> **You:** Iyeh, mzuwwj. 3ndi jooj d lwlad. (Yes, married. I have two kids.)
> **Moroccan:** Tbark Allah! Allah ybarek!

**Translation:**
> **Moroccan:** How old are you?
> **You:** I have 30 years. (= I'm 30)
> **Moroccan:** And married?
> **You:** Yes, married. I have two kids.
> **Moroccan:** God bless! God bless!

## Dialogue 4: Language Exchange

> **You:** Kantkllm ingliziya w shwiya d darija.
> **Moroccan:** Mzyan! Ana kantkllm 3arbiya, fransawiya, w shwiya d ingliziya.
> **You:** 3lash katkel ingliziya mzyan! (But you speak English well!)
> **Moroccan:** Shukran! W nta darija dyalek mzyana!
> **You:** Shukran, mazal. (Thanks, still [learning])

**Translation:**
> **You:** I speak English and a little Darija.
> **Moroccan:** Good! I speak Arabic, French, and a little English.
> **You:** But you speak English well!
> **Moroccan:** Thank you! And your Darija is good!
> **You:** Thanks, still [working on it].

## Cultural Notes

### 1. Moroccans Will Ask You Everything
Don't be surprised if someone you just met asks your age, marital status, salary, and how many kids you have — all in the first conversation. This is NOT considered rude in Morocco. It's genuine interest and warmth. Answer as much as you're comfortable with and smile.

### 2. "Mnin nta?" Is the #1 Question You'll Hear
Moroccans are curious about foreigners. "Where are you from?" will be asked constantly. Have your answer ready: "Ana mn [country]."

### 3. Professions That Impress
Moroccans deeply respect education and professional achievement. Saying you're a doctor, engineer, or teacher will get you extra respect. "Tbark Allah!" will be the response.

### 4. The Language Flex
Most Moroccans speak at least 2-3 languages (Darija, French, sometimes Amazigh/Berber, plus some English or Spanish). Don't be embarrassed about only speaking English. But attempting Darija — even badly — wins HUGE points.

### 5. Gender Endings
Many Darija words change based on gender:
- Male speaker: Khddam (I work), Mzuwwj (married), Mghribi (Moroccan)
- Female speaker: Khddama, Mzuwwja, Mghribiya
General rule: add **-a** for feminine.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Mistake 1: Using "Ismi" Instead of "Smiti"
**Wrong:** "Ismi James" (this is MSA/Standard Arabic)
**Right:** "Smiti James"
In Darija, it's always "smiti." Using "ismi" marks you as a textbook learner.

### ❌ Mistake 2: Forgetting Gender Agreement
**Wrong (if you're female):** "Ana khddam f l'hospital"
**Right:** "Ana khddama f l'hospital"
If you're female, use the feminine form. Moroccans will gently correct you — take it as free learning!

### ❌ Mistake 3: Saying "Ana 3mri..." for Age
**Wrong:** "Ana 3mri tlatin" (I am my-age thirty)
**Right:** "3ndi tlatin 3am" (I have thirty years)
Like French and Spanish, Darija uses "I have [X] years" not "I am [X] years old."

### ❌ Mistake 4: Overcomplicating It
**Wrong:** Trying to conjugate verbs perfectly before speaking.
**Right:** Just say the words. "Ana. James. Amrika. Doctor." Moroccans will understand and appreciate the effort. Grammar comes with practice.

### ❌ Mistake 5: Not Asking Back
**Wrong:** Answering questions without asking "W nta/nti?" (And you?)
**Right:** Always reciprocate. It shows interest and respect.
