# Module 10: Problem Solving — When Things Go Wrong

## Overview
No trip or language journey goes perfectly. You'll get lost, misunderstand someone, need a doctor, or lose your phone. This module prepares you for those moments. These phrases might be the most important ones you learn — they turn a stressful situation into one you can actually handle.

## Dialogue 1: At the Pharmacy

> **You:** Salam, 3ndi moshkil. Wja3ni rassi w 3ndi s-skhana.
> **Pharmacist:** Labas? Men mta w nta hakda?
> **You:** Men lbareh. W 3ndi l-brd shwiya.
> **Pharmacist:** Wakha, ghadi n3tik Doliprane l s-skhana, w had d-dwa l l-brd.
> **You:** Bshhal?
> **Pharmacist:** 35 dirham. Khod jouj 7boub f s-sbah w jouj f l-lil.
> **You:** Shukran bezzaf!
> **Pharmacist:** Bsslama, w rta7 mezyan.

**Translation:**
> **You:** Hello, I have a problem. My head hurts and I have a fever.
> **Pharmacist:** What's wrong? Since when have you been like this?
> **You:** Since yesterday. And I have a bit of a cold.
> **Pharmacist:** Okay, I'll give you Doliprane for the fever, and this medicine for the cold.
> **You:** How much?
> **Pharmacist:** 35 dirhams. Take two pills in the morning and two at night.
> **You:** Thank you so much!
> **Pharmacist:** Take care, and rest well.

## Dialogue 2: Communication Breakdown

> **Moroccan:** *(speaking quickly)* Wa sahbi, ila bghiti tmshi l-had l-blan khask dour men hna w t9lb 3la zenqa li fiha dak l-hanout l-kbir...
> **You:** Smeh li, ma fhemtsh. 3awed 3afak, bshwiya.
> **Moroccan:** *(slower)* Dour... men hna... l-ymen. W sir... nichan.
> **You:** Dour l-ymen, w sir nichan?
> **Moroccan:** Iyeh! W men b3d, ghadi tshuf wahed l-hanout kbir. Temma.
> **You:** Ahhh, fhemt daba! Shukran bezzaf.
> **Moroccan:** Bla jmil, bsslama!

**Translation:**
> **Moroccan:** *(speaking quickly)* Hey friend, if you want to go to that place you need to turn from here and look for the street with the big shop...
> **You:** Sorry, I didn't understand. Repeat please, slowly.
> **Moroccan:** *(slower)* Turn... from here... to the right. And go... straight.
> **You:** Turn right, and go straight?
> **Moroccan:** Yes! And after that, you'll see a big shop. Right there.
> **You:** Ahhh, I understand now! Thank you so much.
> **Moroccan:** You're welcome, take care!

## Dialogue 3: Medical Emergency

> **You:** 3awni 3afak! Sahbti wja3ha bezzaf!
> **Passerby:** Ash kayn? Fin wja3ha?
> **You:** Wja3ha krsha. Ma qaddrash temshi.
> **Passerby:** Khask tmshiw l-sbitar. Ana n3yyet taxi.
> **You:** Fin kayn sbitar qrib?
> **Passerby:** Kayn wahed f 10 dqayq men hna. Aji, yallah!
> *(In the taxi)*
> **You:** L-sbitar 3afak, dghya! 3ndi urgence!
> **Driver:** Wakha, sir hnaya!

**Translation:**
> **You:** Help me please! My friend is in a lot of pain!
> **Passerby:** What's wrong? Where does it hurt?
> **You:** Her stomach hurts. She can't walk.
> **Passerby:** You need to go to the hospital. I'll call a taxi.
> **You:** Where is a nearby hospital?
> **Passerby:** There's one 10 minutes from here. Come on, let's go!
> *(In the taxi)*
> **You:** To the hospital please, quickly! I have an emergency!
> **Driver:** Okay, go that way!

## Dialogue 4: Lost Phone

> **You:** Moshkil kbir! Tlef telephoni!
> **Friend:** Fin khlitih akhir merra?
> **You:** Ma 3reftsh... waqila f taxi, waqila f l-qhwa.
> **Friend:** 3yyet 3lih! — ah, ma 3ndeksh telephon...
> **You:** 3yyet 3lih nta 3afak, men telephonk.
> **Friend:** *(calls)* Rah kaysonni walakin 7edd ma jab.
> **You:** Waqila f taxi. Ash ndir?
> **Friend:** Yallah nmshiw l-mahall dyal taxi, waqila had l-chauffeur rjj3o.
> **You:** W ila ma lqinahsh?
> **Friend:** Nmshiw l-bolice, dir déclaration.

**Translation:**
> **You:** Big problem! My phone is lost!
> **Friend:** Where did you leave it last?
> **You:** I don't know... maybe in the taxi, maybe at the cafe.
> **Friend:** Call it! — oh, you don't have a phone...
> **You:** You call it please, from your phone.
> **Friend:** *(calls)* It's ringing but nobody answered.
> **You:** Maybe in the taxi. What do I do?
> **Friend:** Let's go to the taxi stand, maybe the driver returned it.
> **You:** And if we don't find it?
> **Friend:** We'll go to the police, file a report.

## Cultural Notes

### 🏥 Healthcare in Morocco
Morocco has both public and private hospitals. Private clinics ("clinique") are generally faster and better equipped but cost more. Pharmacies are everywhere and pharmacists can advise on minor ailments without a prescription for many medications.

### 👮 Police and Safety
Morocco is generally very safe for tourists. If you need police, look for officers in blue uniforms or the tourist police in major cities. For emergencies, dial **19** for police, **15** for ambulance, **150** for fire.

### 🤲 People Will Help
Moroccans are incredibly helpful to strangers in trouble. If you say "3awni" in a public place, multiple people will stop. This communal help is deeply cultural — helping someone in need is considered a religious and social duty.

### 💊 Pharmacies Are Medical Advisors
In Morocco, pharmacists do much more than fill prescriptions. They diagnose minor issues, recommend treatments, and sell many medications that would require prescriptions elsewhere. They're your first stop for minor health problems.

### 📱 "Waqila" — The Beautiful Maybe
"Waqila" (maybe/perhaps) is used constantly. It softens statements and avoids absolute certainty. "Waqila ghdda" (maybe tomorrow) is less committal than "ghdda" (tomorrow). This reflects a cultural preference for leaving things open.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Panicking and Switching to English
When stressed, your instinct is to speak English faster and louder. Most Moroccans in medinas don't speak English well. Stay calm, use your Darija basics, and add gestures. "Ma fhemtsh" + pointing + simple words gets you far.

### ❌ Using "Ma fhemtsh" for "I Don't Know"
"Ma fhemtsh" = I didn't understand. "Ma 3reftsh" = I don't know. They're different. If someone asks you a question and you don't know the answer, say "Ma 3reftsh," not "Ma fhemtsh."

### ❌ Being Too Indirect About Medical Issues
In a medical situation, be direct. "Wja3ni hna" (it hurts here) + pointing is perfectly acceptable. Don't try to be polite at the expense of clarity when your health is involved.

### ❌ Assuming "Rah" is Filler
"Rah" is an emphasis word, not meaningless filler. "Rah galha lik" = He DID tell you. "Rah ma kansh" = It actually wasn't there. It adds certainty or correction to a statement.

### ❌ Confusing "Waqila" with "Wakha"
"Waqila" = maybe/perhaps. "Wakha" = okay/agreed. They sound similar but mean completely different things. "Waqila nji" (maybe I'll come) vs. "Wakha, ghadi nji" (okay, I'll come).
