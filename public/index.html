Travel Darija

10 Must-Know Darija Phrases for Travelers

Even knowing a few phrases transforms your Morocco experience — locals light up when you try their language.

Read Guide ↓
  1. Salam (sa-LAHM) — Hello
  2. Shukran bzzaf (shook-RAN b-ZAHF) — Thank you very much
  3. Chhal? (sh-HALL) — How much?
  4. Fin kayna...? (FIN KAY-na) — Where is...?
  5. Bghit hadshi (b-HEIT had-SHI) — I want this
  6. La, shukran (LA shook-RAN) — No, thank you
  7. Wahed atay, afak (WAH-hed a-TAY, a-FAK) — One tea, please
  8. Mezyan bzzaf (mez-YAN b-ZAHF) — Very good
  9. Bslama (b-SLA-ma) — Goodbye
  10. 3afak, fin lhammam? (AH-fak, fin l-ham-MAM) — Excuse me, where is the bathroom?

Tip: Moroccans appreciate any effort to speak Darija — even a single word earns a smile.

Linguistics

Why Darija is NOT Standard Arabic

Many learners are surprised to find they can't understand Moroccan conversations despite knowing Modern Standard Arabic. Here's why.

Read Guide ↓

Darija is a fully independent language, not "bad Arabic." Here's what makes it unique:

  • Amazigh roots: About 40% of vocabulary comes from Tamazight (Berber), the indigenous language of Morocco.
  • French infiltration: Words like "machine" (car), "bureau" (office), and "portable" (phone) are everyday Darija.
  • Spanish echoes: Northern Morocco absorbed words from Spanish — you'll hear traces in food, tools, and daily life.
  • Phonetic compression: Darija contracts heavily. "I don't know" = Ma 3arftsh — classical Arabic words fused and shortened over centuries.
  • No official writing: Darija has no standardized spelling — it's an oral language, often written in "Franco-Arabe" (Latin letters + numbers for Arabic sounds like 3, 7, 9).

Bottom line: if you want to connect with real Moroccans, you need Darija — not MSA.

Culture

Moroccan Culture: What to Know Before Your Trip

Morocco is a country of beautiful contrasts. These cultural insights will help you travel with respect and genuine connection.

Read Guide ↓
  • Tea is a ceremony: Moroccan mint tea (atay) is poured from a height to create foam. Always accept when offered — refusing is considered rude.
  • Dress modestly in medinas: Cover shoulders and knees in markets, mosques, and rural areas. This applies to all genders.
  • Bargaining is expected: In souks, vendors price expecting negotiation. Start at 40% of asking price, smile, and walk away — they'll often call you back.
  • "Inshallah" means maybe: When a Moroccan says "God willing" to confirm plans, treat it as uncertain. It reflects a genuine cultural worldview, not dishonesty.
  • Hospitality is genuine: If invited to a Moroccan home, expect abundant food. Say "no" to a second helping — they'll insist three times, then you accept.
  • Friday rhythms: Many shops close midday Friday for prayers. Plan accordingly in smaller towns and medinas.

Learning even a few Darija phrases before your visit shows respect and opens doors no guidebook can open.
